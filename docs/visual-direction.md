# Sharpened Visual Direction

## Phase

Visual Direction

## Role

Creative Director recommendation for the Sharpened website.

## Scope

This document defines the visual direction for the production website. It covers visual identity, design language, photography, illustrations, icons, layouts, spacing, whitespace, typography mood, and overall feel.

This phase does not create components, UI mockups, React, Tailwind, or code.

## Creative Strategy

Sharpened should look like a focused operations consultancy, not a generic automation agency and not a legal SaaS product.

The visual identity should communicate:

- Operational clarity.
- Professional trust.
- Practical automation.
- SSDI workflow specificity.
- Calm confidence.
- Human-centered systems.

The design should feel structured, precise, and modern, but not cold. The audience is law firm decision-makers and operations leaders, so the visual system needs to earn trust before it tries to impress.

Why:

- SSDI law firm buyers are cautious about workflow, client communication, and operational risk.
- A restrained visual system supports credibility better than decorative tech styling.
- The business needs to differentiate from broad legal platforms by looking consultative and workflow-specific.

How it improves UX:

- Users can scan content quickly and understand hierarchy.
- The visual system reduces cognitive load.
- Visitors are guided toward the Workflow Assessment without feeling pushed.

How it improves conversion:

- A serious, clear visual tone reduces trust friction.
- Yellow CTAs become unmistakable conversion signals.
- Workflow diagrams and realistic visuals make the offer easier to understand.

How it improves maintainability:

- Clear visual rules prevent one-off design decisions.
- Future pages can reuse the same layout and visual logic.
- The brand palette remains disciplined.

How it improves scalability:

- The direction can support resources, case studies, industry pages, and future frameworks without redesigning the brand.

## Brand Color Direction

Approved colors:

| Role | Hex | Creative Use |
|---|---|---|
| Primary | `#00153B` | Authority, structure, headers, footer, high-trust bands |
| Secondary | `#0642D0` | Interactive detail, links, diagrams, active states |
| Accent | `#FEC001` | Primary CTAs only |
| Background | `#F0F1F4` | Soft page canvas and muted section bands |
| Text | `#353637` | Primary readable content |

## Color Personality

### Navy

Navy should carry authority and calm. Use it for major structural moments: header, footer, strong trust sections, and selected high-emphasis blocks.

### Blue

Blue should signal clarity, process, and interactivity. Use it for links, active states, workflow diagram lines, and small technical accents.

### Yellow

Yellow must remain a conversion color. It should not be used decoratively, as a large background, or as a general highlight color.

### Soft Gray

Soft gray should create quiet separation between content sections without making the page feel cold.

### Text Gray

Text gray should keep reading comfortable and professional. Avoid pure black unless needed for accessibility in specific contexts.

## Design Language

## Recommended Design Language

The design language should be "structured operational clarity."

This means:

- Every section should have a clear purpose.
- Layouts should feel ordered and calm.
- Visual accents should explain process or guide action.
- Space should create confidence, not emptiness.
- The site should feel like a partner that understands operations.

## Visual Motifs

Use motifs that connect directly to Sharpened's work:

- Workflow paths.
- Process steps.
- Document checklists.
- Handoff points.
- Status markers.
- Assessment findings.
- Before/after workflow comparisons.
- System connection diagrams.

Do not use motifs that imply generic tech:

- Floating abstract screens.
- Random connected dots with no meaning.
- Futuristic AI grids.
- Stock dashboard filler.
- Decorative geometric shapes without function.

It should not feel:

## Photography Direction

## Photography Role

Photography should support credibility and human context. It should not be the main proof of the offer. Workflow visuals and sample artifacts should carry more strategic weight than stock photography.

Recommended photography subjects:

- Professional service teams working at desks.
- Operations staff reviewing workflow or case information.
- Remote consulting or collaborative work sessions.
- Laptops with realistic business context.
- Organized workspaces.
- Hands reviewing process documents.
- Small team discussion around a screen.

Photography mood:

- Bright.
- Clean.
- Calm.
- Professional.
- Realistic.
- Warm enough to feel human.

Avoid:

- Gavels.
- Courtrooms.
- Judges.
- Scales of justice as primary imagery.
- Distressed clients.
- Medical imagery.
- Dark hacker-style automation visuals.
- Overly posed corporate stock photos.
- Images that imply specific claimants, attorneys, or client outcomes without proper rights.

Why:

- UX improves because imagery supports context without distracting from the message.
- Conversion improves because realistic operational visuals feel credible.
- Maintainability improves because the image rules are specific and reusable.
- Scalability improves because future pages can use the same photography library direction.

## Photography Treatment

Recommended treatment:

- Natural color temperature.
- Moderate contrast.
- Clean cropping.
- Clear subject focus.
- No heavy overlays.
- No dark photo backgrounds behind long copy.
- Minimal color grading.

If using photography in hero areas:

- Do not obscure the main headline.
- Do not rely on darkened stock photos to create drama.
- Prefer a supporting photo beside or below the message, unless a real high-quality brand image exists.

## Illustration Direction

## Illustration Role

Illustrations should explain workflow, not decorate.

Recommended illustration types:

- Workflow diagrams.
- Assessment roadmap visuals.
- Intake-to-filing process maps.
- Before/after workflow comparisons.
- Bottleneck maps.
- Task handoff diagrams.
- Automation trigger examples.
- Document collection flow.

Illustration style:

- Simple line-based diagrams.
- Mostly navy, blue, gray, and white.
- Yellow only for action points or CTA-adjacent emphasis.
- Clear labels.
- Minimal decorative detail.
- Functional over expressive.

Avoid:

- Character illustrations.
- Abstract people floating around dashboards.
- Generic AI sparkles.
- Complex diagrams that require explanation.
- Decorative icon clusters with no operational meaning.

Why:

- UX improves because diagrams make services easier to understand.
- Conversion improves because the Workflow Assessment becomes more tangible.
- Maintainability improves because diagrams can be reused in sales decks and proposals.
- Scalability improves because future frameworks can become visual assets.

## Icon Direction

## Icon Style

Use outline icons with:

- Rounded corners.
- Consistent stroke width.
- Simple geometry.
- Clear meaning.
- Minimal detail.

Recommended icon themes:

- Workflow.
- Calendar.
- Checklist.
- File text.
- Message.
- Clock.
- Route.
- Shield.
- Clipboard.
- Send.
- Settings.
- Users.
- Arrow right.

Icon use by content area:

- Services: one icon per service card.
- Process: numbered steps should be primary; icons optional.
- Trust: shield, checklist, file text, route, users.
- FAQ: minimal or no icons.
- CTAs: arrow or calendar icon only when useful.

Avoid:

- Mixed icon styles.
- Filled icons mixed with outline icons.
- Emojis.
- Decorative icon clouds.
- Icons that require interpretation.

Why:

- UX improves because icons support scanning.
- Conversion improves because key actions are visually reinforced.
- Maintainability improves because one icon style keeps the system consistent.
- Scalability improves because new services can use the same icon logic.

## Layout Direction

## Layout Philosophy

Layouts should feel editorially clear and operationally structured. The site should guide the visitor through a decision path, not scatter equal-weight cards across every section.

Recommended layout behavior:

- Mobile-first single-column clarity.
- Desktop sections with strong left-to-right reading flow.
- Alternating full-width bands and white sections.
- Clear section intros.
- Content grouped by user question.
- Cards only for repeated items.
- Process steps displayed as ordered sequences.
- Trust sections given enough breathing room.

Avoid:

- Cards inside cards.
- Floating section containers everywhere.
- Dense SaaS dashboards as decorative fillers.
- Overly complex asymmetry.
- Hero layouts that hide the next section.
- Multi-column long text on mobile.

## Homepage Layout Direction

Recommended visual flow:

1. Hero with clear message and CTA.
2. Problem section using concise pain cards or grouped statements.
3. Solution section using an assessment-led process visual.
4. Services preview using controlled card grid.
5. Process section using numbered steps.
6. Trust section with founder-market fit and privacy reassurance.
7. FAQ accordion or grouped questions.
8. Final CTA band.

Creative note:

- The homepage should feel like a guided consultation, not a product tour.

## Services Layout Direction

## Spacing Direction

Spacing should feel generous but purposeful.

Recommended approach:

- Use strong vertical section rhythm.
- Keep related content tight enough to scan.
- Give CTAs enough surrounding space to stand out.
- Use whitespace to separate decision stages.
- Avoid large empty gaps that make the site feel unfinished.

Spacing personality:

- Calm.
- Ordered.
- Breathing room.
- Not luxury editorial.
- Not dense enterprise software.

Why:

- UX improves because each decision stage is easier to process.
- Conversion improves because CTAs get visual priority.
- Maintainability improves because spacing rules can be reused.
- Scalability improves because future pages can maintain the same rhythm.

## Whitespace Direction

Whitespace should be treated as a trust tool.

Use whitespace to:

- Separate problem, solution, service, trust, and CTA moments.
- Improve scan speed.
- Make forms feel less intimidating.
- Let workflow diagrams breathe.
- Prevent service cards from feeling like a product catalog.

Avoid:

- Crammed card grids.
- Overloaded hero content.
- Crowded navigation.
- FAQ sections with insufficient vertical rhythm.
- Dense paragraphs across full-width containers.

## Typography Mood

Recommended typography mood:

- Strong.
- Clear.
- Professional.
- Modern.
- Calm.
- Direct.

Heading direction:

- Use strong, confident headings.
- Avoid overly expressive display styling.
- Keep headings compact and easy to scan.
- Use hero-scale type only for hero sections.

Body direction:

- Body text should feel practical and readable.
- Avoid tiny text in trust or form areas.
- Keep paragraph width moderate.
- Prefer plain, helpful language.

Typography should not feel:

- Luxury.
- Legal-traditional.
- Playful startup.
- Overly technical.
- Editorial magazine.

Why:

- UX improves because typography supports quick understanding.
- Conversion improves because copy feels credible and direct.
- Maintainability improves because type roles are clear.
- Scalability improves because future content can follow the same hierarchy.

## Visual Hierarchy

Priority order:

1. Page purpose.
2. Primary CTA.
3. SSDI-specific problem.
4. Workflow-first method.
5. Services.
6. Trust and privacy.
7. FAQ and secondary links.

Visual rules:

- The primary CTA should be the only yellow element in a section.
- Section headings should not compete with hero headings.
- Service cards should be equal-weight unless one is intentionally featured.
- Trust statements should be visually quieter than CTAs but stronger than supporting copy.
- FAQ should support conversion, not dominate the page.

## Trust Visual System

Trust should be shown through structure, not badges alone.

Recommended trust visuals:

- Founder experience block.
- SHARP values grid.
- Privacy-safe inquiry note.
- Assessment deliverables list.
- Workflow map preview.
- Documentation / handoff visual.
- Current-tools reassurance.

Avoid:

- Fake logos.
- Generic review badges.
- Unverified metrics.
- Stock security claims.
- Compliance claims that are not legally confirmed.

Why:

- UX improves because trust feels specific and honest.
- Conversion improves because risk is reduced without exaggeration.
- Maintainability improves because trust blocks can be updated as proof grows.
- Scalability improves because testimonials and case studies can later replace placeholders.

## CTA Visual Direction

Primary CTA:

- Yellow background: `#FEC001`.
- Navy or dark text after contrast validation.
- Strong label.
- Enough surrounding whitespace.
- Used sparingly.

Secondary CTA:

- Text or outline treatment.
- Navy or blue.
- Lower visual weight than primary CTA.

CTA behavior:

- Each major section can include one action.
- CTA placement should follow user readiness.
- Repeated CTAs should feel contextual, not aggressive.

Avoid:

- Multiple yellow buttons in one visual cluster.
- Yellow decorative icons near CTAs.
- Vague CTA labels.
- CTA overload in navigation.

## Recommended Creative Direction

The recommended direction is:

> Professional workflow clarity for SSDI law firm operations.

This direction should use:

- Navy for trust.
- Blue for workflow clarity.
- Yellow only for action.
- White and soft gray for breathable structure.
- Realistic professional photography.
- Functional workflow diagrams.

This direction is stronger than a generic SaaS style because Sharpened is not selling a platform. It is selling workflow clarity, diagnosis, implementation judgment, and trust.

## Creative Risks

## Risk 1: Looking Too Generic

If the site uses abstract automation visuals, it will look like any automation agency.

Recommendation:

- Use SSDI workflow examples, process diagrams, and assessment visuals.

## Risk 2: Looking Too Legal

If the site leans on gavels, courtrooms, and traditional legal imagery, it will feel dated and less operational.

Recommendation:

- Use professional operations imagery and workflow visuals instead.

## Risk 3: Looking Too SaaS

If the site uses dashboard-heavy visuals and feature grids, prospects may assume Sharpened is software.

Recommendation:

- Show process, assessment, and services as consulting-led workflow improvement.

## Risk 4: Weak CTA Hierarchy

If yellow is used decoratively, CTA clarity weakens.

Recommendation:

- Reserve yellow for primary actions only.

## Risk 5: Insufficient Trust

If no proof assets exist, the site may feel untested.

Recommendation:

- Use founder experience, sample workflow artifacts, privacy notes, and clear deliverables until testimonials exist.

## Final Visual Direction Summary

Sharpened should look like a calm, precise, modern workflow consultancy for SSDI law firms. The visual system should use structure, whitespace, typography, workflow diagrams, and restrained color to create trust.

The site should not chase flashy automation aesthetics. It should make the visitor feel that Sharpened understands operational complexity, can simplify it, and has a clear first step: the Workflow Assessment.

No components, UI mockups, React, Tailwind, or code were created in this phase.
